Life Alert Redesign

The Problem

Redesign the digital offerings of Life Alert and improve their system to meet 21st century competition. 

Challenge #1:  People wait until their first fall

Research

32% of Americans age 85 and older suffer from Alzheimer’s disease.

Solution

Care Circles

  • One of the big problems we identified was the attractiveness of the neck pendant. People were resistant to wear it because they thought it was ugly and was also associated with being old. We’re recommending that Life Alert integrate with modern fitness trackers, and they can also design their own as retail prices for wearables can be in the $10-20 range for the needed functionality.
  • Another problem we identified is that people are unaware of the dangers of falling until they have their first bad fall. Education, prevention, and balance exercises can go a long way to minimize falls and mitigate these risks.
  • Combined with earlier adoption from a more attractive device, we redesigned the website to include education about fall prevention.
  • We found that hardly any people surveyed had smartphones, and many elderly people don’t have a clear reason to buy one. Because of this, the interface was designed to be extremely simple, straightforward, and have a lot of clear affordances around buttons.
  • We were surprised to find one of the most common concerns for the elderly was loneliness and depression. Because of this, we focused on messaging features to make the elderly feel more connected to their families, and we designed a voice-based system, as that’s how they prefer to communicate and voice interactions can be a lot more heartfelt.

  • Finally, we found a drop-off in usage ability once people were overcome with Dementia. To fix this, we’re recommending a system with proactive fall detection and passive monitoring so the user doesn’t have to push any buttons for the system to activate.

 

The best UI is no UI.

 

Because our users potentially suffer from many ailments, both mental and physical, we have recommended that the system have a no UI approach. Speakers and microphones are placed around the house, and when the system detects that the user has fallen, it automatically contacts Life Alert and gets a dispatcher on the line. Given the difficulties and situations where the user might not be able or not remember to use the system, we're recommending a smarter approach that uses all the information from sensors to automatically detect and call dispatchers if an event is triggered.

Results & Reflections

Good empathic design asks you to think about the context of your users. In the case of Life Alert, their users are elderly people most of which haven't used a smartphone, and even those that have are suffering from physical ailments such as Alzheimers, mental ailments such as Dementia, and emotional ailments like loneliness and depression. The design needs to reflect that reality and make the user experience as simple and straightforward as possible, and each screen needs to stand on its own with the understanding that the user might not remember how they got there. More usability testing needs to be done, especially focused on users that are 80+ years old to make sure the experience is completely intuitive and as straightforward as possible. Life Alert has a huge opportunity to leverage the trend of activity and fitness trackers and create a system that looks like jewelry, makes users healthier, and helps our grandparents feel connected.
